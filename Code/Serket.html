Serket

Serket is a benevolent scorpion goddess according to the Pyramid Texts she was the mother of Nehebkau, the snake god who protected the pharaoh from snakebites. She was closely associated with Isis and her sister Nephthys because of her connection with magic and the underworld. In Edfu she was said to be the wife of Horus and the mother of Horakhty (Horus of the Horizon) and was viewed as a protective goddess but she also had a darker side. Serket was also thought to have power over venomous snakes and scorpions, like Meretseger and Isis. Serket was believed to be able to protect a person from venomous bites and could even send out snakes and scorpions to punish whom she disapproved of. Serket was first worshiped originally in the Delta but her popularity spread throughout the land and cult centers were established at Djeba and Per-Serqet, however no temples specifically dedicated to her have ever been found. According to Serket’s story and other versions Serket Help protect Isis and her infant son Horus when they were hiding from Set in the marshes of the Delta. Serket is associated with the seven scorpions, who act as a bodyguard for the mother and child and is sometimes named as the goddess who convinces Ra to stop the sun barque and help when Hours is poisoned by a magical snake sent by Set. Because of this story, she was thought to offer special protection to children and pregnant women from venomous animals. Serket also protects Ra whenever he travels throughout the underworld each night. She was said to watch over any dangerous twists in the path and was responsible for restraining the serpent Apep when he was imprisoned in the netherworld and protecting the sun god’s barqe from his attack. This protection was extended to everyone who made this difficult journey through the netherworld. Serket was associated with the western cardinal point (the west was associated with death and rebirth). Yet, she was closely associated with the breath of life. Her enemies would have their breath literally taken away by the effect of a poisonous bite, but she also gave the justified dead the breath of life in the afterworld.1
